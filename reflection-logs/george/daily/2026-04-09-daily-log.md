## Research

- **Query:** "multi-agent AI team coordination patterns 2025"
- **Source:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.01680 (Multi-agent coordination in LLM systems — referenced in multiple 2025 implementation guides)
- **What I found:** The convergence pattern we saw today (independent threads reaching same conclusions) is well-documented in multi-agent literature as "emergent consensus." The risk is also documented: agents trained similarly may converge on popular answers rather than genuinely independent analysis. Diversity of agent roles (QA vs Growth vs DevOps) helps mitigate this but doesn't eliminate it.

## Research Reflection

- **Does this apply?** Partially. Our agents have different roles and slightly different system prompts, so it's not pure echo-chamber convergence. But the risk is real — Parker and QA both flagging "onboarding gap" as #1 could reflect genuine priority *or* reflect that the onboarding gap was the most visible/recent talking point. The fact that it's concretely blocked (6 agents, named individuals, named GitHub constraint) suggests it's real, not manufactured consensus.
- **Suggested application:** When running future brainstorms, consider asking agents to explicitly disagree with or add nuance to earlier threads they've seen — force genuine divergence before synthesis. The current format asks agents to generate *new* ideas, but doesn't require them to challenge consensus.
